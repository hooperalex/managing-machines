# Turn the Ship Around! — L. David Marquet's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Turn the Ship Around!* (L. David Marquet, 2012). This skill lets you **apply**
Marquet's intent-based leadership system, not just describe it. Extracted by Managing
Machines, Issue 013.

## When to use
- The user is the bottleneck — every decision routes through them — and wants to push it down.
- A delegation / autonomy question: how much authority to hand a person or an agent, and how fast.
- Designing approval gates, review steps, or "plan-and-confirm" flows.
- Trying to convert passive order-takers into active owners.
- Diagnosing why an empowerment push created chaos (usually: control without competence or clarity).

## Core principle
**Don't move information to authority — move authority to the information.** The person
(or agent) closest to the problem holds the freshest context. Stop dragging decisions up
the chain; push decision rights down to where the knowledge already is. Replace
leader-follower (some command, the rest comply) with **leader-leader** (everyone leads
where they stand). But authority is only safe when it rides on **competence** and **clarity**.

## The frameworks

**The Leader-Leader Model** — design the org so everyone exercises leadership, not just the
top. *Procedure:* find the places where people wait to be told; identify the decision they
could own; transfer that decision right explicitly. *Use when* people are passive, initiative
is dead, or you're the single point of decision.

**Intent-Based Leadership ("I intend to…")** — the signature mechanism. *Procedure:* ban
"Can I…?" / "Should we…?"; require people to state "I intend to <action>, because
<reasoning>." The leader responds with a nod or a clarifying question. *Use when* you want
ownership and a cheap checkpoint before action. It forces the speaker to think a level up.

**Control → Competence → Clarity** — give control, but gated by the other two. *Procedure:*
before widening someone's authority, ask: do they have the technical *competence* for this
decision, and the *clarity* (shared goal + guiding principles) to decide the way you would?
Build the missing pillar first. *Use when* deciding how much, and how fast, to delegate.

**Deliberate Action** — pause and vocalize intent before acting. *Procedure:* require a
visible "I am about to do X" beat before consequential moves; this catches errors and
auto-corrects. *Use when* mistakes come from acting on autopilot.

**Thinking Out Loud** — narrate reasoning, not just conclusions. *Procedure:* have people
(and yourself) speak the *why* behind a call so context is shared and others can intercept a
bad assumption. *Use when* context is siloed or you need others to catch your mistakes.

**Don't Brief, Certify** — replace passive briefings with active certification. *Procedure:*
instead of telling the team what will happen, make the team prove to you it's ready — restate
the plan, surface risks, confirm prep. *Use when* preparation is sloppy or ownership is thin.

**Specify Goals, Not Methods** — say where to go, let them choose how. *Procedure:* state the
outcome and the constraints; resist supplying the solution. *Use when* you catch yourself
prescribing steps a competent person/agent could choose themselves.

## How to apply
A decision procedure — run it top to bottom each time you face a delegation call.
1. **Spot the bottleneck.** Where do decisions queue on you? Name the specific decision right.
2. **Locate the information.** Who/what is closest to the relevant context? That's the new owner.
3. **Check the two gates.** Competence: can they do it? Clarity: do they share the goal/principles?
   If either is missing, build it before transferring authority.
4. **Switch to intent.** Have them say "I intend to… because…"; you nod or question. Don't solve for them.
5. **Add deliberate action + think out loud** for consequential moves, so errors surface pre-action.
6. **Certify, don't brief.** Make readiness something they prove back to you.
7. **Specify the goal, release the method.** State outcome + constraints; let them choose how.
8. **Widen authority as competence and clarity grow.** Excellence as the standard, not error-avoidance.

## In the agentic world order
- **Move authority to the information.** The agent holds the freshest context (files, test
  output, traces). Don't funnel every call through a human gate — push the decision to the
  agent and reserve yourself for setting intent and reviewing results.
- **"I intend to…" = plan-and-confirm.** Have the agent declare its plan ("I intend to refactor
  X, run Y, then deploy Z") before acting. It turns a command-taker into a planner and gives
  you a cheap checkpoint where mistakes are cheap to fix.
- **Control gated by competence + clarity.** Competence = evals, tests, tool access proving the
  agent can do the task. Clarity = a system prompt stating guiding principles and the end goal.
  Grant autonomy only as fast as both are real; don't widen scope on an unproven agent.
- **Certify, don't brief.** Make the agent restate the plan and list what it will touch before it
  runs — readiness proven back to you, not context dumped at it.
- **Deliberate action / think out loud → reasoning traces.** Require the agent to state intent and
  reasoning before writing, so errors appear in the plan, not in production.
- **Specify goals, not methods.** Give outcome + constraints in the prompt and let the agent choose
  the approach; prescribe steps only where competence or safety is unproven.

## Pitfalls
- **Author's own caution: control without competence/clarity is chaos.** Empowerment that runs
  ahead of skill and shared purpose produces dangerous freelancing — build the two pillars first.
- **Author's own caution: avoid running on error-avoidance.** A culture optimized to not screw up
  goes silent and brittle; aim for excellence so people lean in.
- **It's one case study generalized.** A single all-male late-90s submarine crew; the mechanisms
  transfer but the evidence base is narrow — validate, don't assume universality.
- **Intent-declaring isn't culturally neutral.** Direct, assertive "I intend to…" behavior is
  rewarded unevenly — it can land differently for women or in cultures that don't reward it. Watch
  whose intent gets weight, and design for equal voice.
- **The ideas borrow from Covey's 7 Habits** and the chunked self-help packaging can make them feel
  more novel than they are — keep the substance, ignore the marketing format.
- **Don't cargo-cult the mechanisms.** "I intend to…" or "certify don't brief" are tools, not magic
  words; transfer the underlying authority, or you've just added ritual on top of leader-follower.
