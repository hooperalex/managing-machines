# Sprint — Jake Knapp's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Sprint: How to Solve Big Problems and Test New Ideas in Just Five Days*
(Jake Knapp with John Zeratsky & Braden Kowitz, 2016). This skill lets you **run**
a sprint, not just describe one. Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 009.

## When to use
- A big, important, fuzzy problem where the team disagrees on direction.
- You're about to commit serious time/money and want evidence first.
- A decision is stuck in meetings, opinions, and analysis-paralysis.
- You need to test whether customers actually understand and want an idea.
- The agentic equivalent: choosing a workflow/architecture, or de-risking an
  approach before building it across a fleet.

## Core principle
**Don't debate — build a realistic prototype and put it in front of real customers,
all inside one time-boxed week.** Compress months of cycle time into five days by
combining a small team, a clear structure, individual work, decisive authority, and
a cheap disposable test. You buy data before you buy commitment.

## The frameworks

**The Five-Day Sprint** — a fixed arc that times the whole effort.
*Procedure:* Monday map the problem + pick a target; Tuesday sketch solutions;
Wednesday decide + storyboard; Thursday build the prototype; Friday test with
customers. *Use when* you have a big problem and can clear a focused block of time.

**Set the Stage** — get the conditions right before you start.
*Procedure:* pick a challenge big/risky enough to deserve a sprint; recruit a team of
7 or fewer spanning the needed expertise (plus a "Facilitator" to run time and a
"Decider" to own calls); book one room with whiteboards for the full week, 10–5 daily;
ban laptops/phones except during designated breaks. *Use when* assembling a sprint.

**Start at the End** — aim the week at the riskiest unknowns.
*Procedure:* write the long-term goal; list the sprint questions ("can we…?") and the
dangerous assumptions; make a simple map of the problem. *Use when* a sprint or
project kicks off — define success and risk before generating ideas.

**Together Alone (Four-Step Sketch)** — replace brainstorming with structured solo work.
*Procedure:* (1) take notes around the room, (2) jot rough ideas, (3) Crazy 8s — eight
variations in eight minutes, (4) a detailed, self-explanatory, anonymous solution
sketch per person. Seed with Lightning Demos (3-min demos of existing inspiration).
*Use when* you need deep, diverse ideas without loud-voice groupthink.

**The Decider & Structured Decision** — converge without endless debate.
*Procedure:* hang sketches (Art Museum); silent review with heat-map dot stickers;
quick speed critique; a straw poll; then the Decider makes the binding call (a Rumble
if two ideas both deserve testing). Capture the winner as a step-by-step Storyboard.
*Use when* choosing among options — one accountable Decider, not consensus.

**Prototype Mindset (Goldilocks "Fake It")** — build only enough to learn.
*Procedure:* adopt a prototype mindset (disposable, build-to-learn); choose tools you
can move fast in; create a realistic *façade* with just enough quality to evoke honest
reactions; divide roles (makers, stitcher, writer, asker) and finish in a day; do a
trial run. *Use when* you need a testable artifact cheaply.

**Five-Act Interview** — get real signal from real people.
*Procedure:* recruit ~5 target customers (screen with a Craigslist-style survey); run
each as a one-on-one in five acts — (1) friendly welcome, (2) context questions about
their life/work, (3) introduce the prototype, (4) tasks while they think aloud, (5)
quick debrief; the team watches live in another room and fills a grid of notes; after
all five, find patterns. ~5 users expose roughly 85% of usability problems (Nielsen).
*Use when* validating whether the idea actually lands with the people you built it for.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Qualify it.** Is this big, risky, and worth a focused week? If not, don't sprint.
2. **Set the stage.** Small team, the right experts, a single Decider, cleared time/space.
3. **Start at the end.** Write the goal + sprint questions + assumptions; map the problem; pick one target.
4. **Diverge solo.** Lightning Demos, then the four-step sketch — individual work, then share.
5. **Decide.** Art Museum → heat map → speed critique → straw poll → Decider's call → Storyboard.
6. **Fake it.** Build a Goldilocks-quality, disposable façade in a day; trial-run it.
7. **Test & learn.** Five-act interviews with ~5 target users; find patterns; decide the next move.

## In the agentic world order
- **Five-day sprint → the agent spike.** The loop is the same, just faster: don't argue
  whether a workflow works — have agents prototype it and test it against real cases the
  same day. Time-box it so you ship a decision, not an open investigation.
- **Start at the end → write the eval first.** Encode the goal and sprint questions as an
  eval before agents generate anything. The riskiest assumption becomes the pass/fail test,
  not a debate in review.
- **Together alone → parallel agents, then converge.** Run several agents independently on
  the same problem (the fleet's Crazy 8s) rather than one consensus mega-prompt; diverse
  drafts surface stronger options. Then decide.
- **Decider stays human.** Authority to choose, kill, and ship is the one role you never
  delegate. Use structured convergence (heat map / straw poll over agent outputs) to inform
  the call — but a human makes it.
- **Goldilocks prototype → disposable agent builds.** Have agents build a façade just real
  enough to test, and treat it as throwaway. Optimize for honest signal from users/data, not
  a polished system you've grown attached to.
- **Five-act interview → test on real cases.** Validate against ~5 real target scenarios and
  watch behavior, not just "did it run." Don't mistake "the agent produced output" for "the
  output is what the user actually needs."

## Pitfalls
- **Knapp's own cautions:** a sprint needs a *real* Decider with authority — without one it
  collapses into mush. Pick a challenge worthy of the time. Keep prototypes Goldilocks-quality
  and disposable; over-building wastes the week and biases you against killing it.
- **One-size-fits-all risk:** the format fits surface/UX/product-direction questions far better
  than deep technical, research-heavy, or infrastructure problems. Don't force every problem
  into five days.
- **Usability ≠ desirability:** five customers understanding a prototype is not proof they want
  it. Design the test to probe genuine need, not just task completion.
- **The week ends, then what?** The book is light on sustaining momentum and folding findings
  into normal org culture — plan the follow-through yourself; a sprint result is a start, not a
  finished product.
- **Thin on rigor:** it's a practitioner playbook, light on citations and statistical proof.
  Treat results as strong directional signal, not conclusive data.
