# The Making of a Manager — Julie Zhuo's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *The Making of a Manager* (Julie Zhuo, 2019). This skill lets you **apply**
Zhuo's first-time-manager playbook, not just describe it. Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 018.

## When to use
- The user just became a manager (of people or of agents) and feels in over their head.
- Leading a small team: building trust, running 1:1s, giving feedback.
- A feedback / expectations problem: someone (or some agent) keeps missing the mark.
- Hiring, or designing a role and a bar for "great."
- Scaling: delegating as the team grows, and setting culture deliberately.
- Managing yourself: impostor feelings, knowing your triggers, asking for feedback on you.

## Core principle
**A manager's job is to get better outcomes from a group of people working together by
improving its purpose, people, and process.** You are a multiplier, not the top individual
contributor. Measure yourself by the team's collective outcome, not your personal output.

## The frameworks

**Purpose · People · Process** — the three levers a manager pulls. *Procedure:* diagnose
which lever is weak. **Purpose** — does the team know what success looks like and care about
it? Fix by clarifying the goal and the why. **People** — do they have the skills and
motivation to thrive? Fix by coaching, support, or role changes. **Process** — how do they
decide and work together? Fix the meeting/decision/handoff that's broken. *Use when* outcomes
are off and you need to find the root cause instead of just working harder.

**The Four Paths to Management** — name how you arrived, because each has a different trap.
*Procedure:* identify your path → **Apprentice** (boss split the team): you know the work,
risk is acting like a peer. **Pioneer** (you founded it): risk is never letting go. **New
boss** (joined from outside): risk is changing things before earning trust. **Successor**
(your manager left): risk is either copying them or overcorrecting. Pick the matching first-90-days
focus. *Use when* starting a new management role.

**The Feedback Model** — feedback is a gift given early and often. *Procedure:* (1) set
crystal-clear expectations up front so there are no surprises; (2) give feedback that is
task-specific and frequent, behavior-focused (what they did, not who they are), and kindly
delivered; (3) gather 360° — up, down, and sideways. *Use when* performance is drifting or a
review is looming. The goal is course-correction in real time, not a saved-up verdict.

**The First Three Months** — learn the job in public. *Procedure:* expect to feel like an
impostor (normal); listen and ask before changing things; build trust by being honest about
what you don't know; find quick wins that help the team, not your ego. *Use when* in a
management transition.

**Managing Yourself** — you can't lead others past where you've led yourself. *Procedure:*
audit your strengths and your triggers; ask your team and peers for feedback on *your*
performance; reframe impostor syndrome as a learning posture, not a verdict on your worth.
*Use when* you're the bottleneck, or doubt is running the show.

**Hiring Well** — hiring is the work, not an interruption to it. *Procedure:* design the role
and write a portrait of what "great" looks like before sourcing; interview for that bar;
treat every hire as a long-term bet on the team you want in a year. *Use when* growing the
team or filling a gap.

**Nurturing Culture** — culture is what the team rewards, tolerates, and punishes. *Procedure:*
decide the few values that matter; reinforce them through what you celebrate and what you
refuse to accept; model them yourself, because the team copies behavior, not slogans. *Use
when* the team is scaling past your direct reach.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Reframe to outcome.** Restate the goal as the team's collective outcome, not your tasks.
2. **Diagnose the lever.** Is the gap in purpose, people, or process? Fix the right one.
3. **Set expectations first.** Make "what good looks like" explicit before judging anyone.
4. **Give feedback fast.** Specific, behavior-focused, frequent, 360° — not saved for review.
5. **Calibrate to the person.** Match support vs autonomy to their skill and motivation.
6. **Hire and delegate for growth.** Build the bar; hand off the work the team can own.
7. **Set culture deliberately.** Reward the behavior you want; model it yourself.
8. **Manage yourself in parallel.** Know your triggers; ask for feedback on your own leadership.

## In the agentic world order
- **Purpose / people / process for a fleet.** *Purpose:* a crisp definition of done and a goal
  the agent can't drift from. *People:* the right model, tools, and context for the task.
  *Process:* how runs get reviewed, escalated, and merged. Most fleet failures are a process
  gap, not a model gap.
- **Expectations before feedback = spec + eval.** You can't course-correct an agent against a
  standard you never wrote down. Zhuo's "set clear expectations first" is the spec; her
  frequent behavior-focused feedback is the eval suite and the prompt diff.
- **"Know what success looks like" → the rubric.** Encode success into the system prompt and
  grading rubric so the agent optimizes the real outcome, not a proxy it can game.
- **Four paths → how you inherited the fleet.** Built it (pioneer), took it over (successor),
  joined a stack (new boss): adjust how much you change before you've earned trust in its behavior.
- **Hiring → model/tool selection.** Designing the role and the bar = picking the model, tools,
  and guardrails for the job, against an explicit portrait of "great."
- **Culture → guardrails.** Culture is what runs the team when you're not watching — for a fleet
  that's the values, constraints, and refusal behavior baked into the prompt and tooling.

## Pitfalls
- **The honest-feedback gap (Zhuo's own warning).** Power dynamics make reports hesitate to
  challenge the boss; an agent is worse — it will cheerfully report success. Build the 360°
  / independent check that surfaces the truth instead of trusting the self-report.
- **Soft over substance.** The early chapters lean psychological; don't let warmth substitute
  for a concrete spec, metric, or decision. Pair the empathy with a hard definition of done.
- **One-context bias.** The book is drawn almost entirely from one fast-growing big-tech
  setting and never critiques it. Translate the advice to your context; don't assume your
  constraints (regulated, distributed, low-trust, agentic) match Facebook's.
- **Diminishing returns.** Some chapters (e.g. meetings) restate the basics. Take the strong
  frameworks (purpose/people/process, feedback, hiring, culture) and don't over-index on the
  thin ones.
