# Remote: Office Not Required — Fried & Hansson's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Remote: Office Not Required* (Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson, 2013).
This skill lets you **apply** Basecamp's remote-work operating system, not just describe it.
Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 027.

## When to use
- The user is moving a team (or a fleet of agents) to distributed / async work.
- A supervision question: how to manage when you can't see anyone "working."
- Designing collaboration: how much real-time overlap vs. async/written work.
- Hiring remotely: where to source, how to test, how to pay.
- Diagnosing remote failure modes: isolation, overwork, weak culture, security.

## Core principle
**Manage the work, not the chairs.** Presence is not productivity. Restricting talent
(or models, or tools) to what's physically nearby leaves the best options on the table.
Judge people and agents by their output, give them long uninterrupted stretches, and
replace surveillance with trust plus visible results.

## The frameworks

**Talent Isn't Bound by the Hubs** — the best person is rarely within commuting distance.
*Procedure:* drop the geography filter; source candidates globally; evaluate on output and
writing, not location. *Use when* hiring, or choosing any resource you've defaulted to
"local" out of habit.

**Work Doesn't Happen at Work (the M&Ms)** — Managers and Meetings are the two biggest
interruptions. *Procedure:* protect long blocks of uninterrupted time; convert status
meetings to written async updates; treat meetings as seasoning, used sparingly. *Use when*
output is fragmented by interruptions.

**Stop Managing the Chairs** — manage the product, not seat-time. *Procedure:* define what
"done/good" looks like as an artifact; review the artifact, not the hours; if you can't tell
work is happening without watching, fix the goals/metrics, not the visibility. *Use when*
tempted to monitor presence.

**Trust as a Precondition** — "Either learn to trust the people you're working with, or find
some other people." *Procedure:* hire people you don't need to watch; grant autonomy by
default; if trust is impossible, that's a hiring problem, not a remote problem. *Use when*
the instinct is to add surveillance.

**Overlap, Then Async** — collaboration needs a shared window, not a shared schedule.
*Procedure:* secure ~4 hours of overlap for real-time work; do the rest asynchronously and
in writing, with durable records; share screens to transfer context. *Use when* designing
how a distributed team communicates.

**The Virtual Water Cooler** — culture and casual connection must be engineered remotely.
*Procedure:* stand up a persistent, low-stakes shared space (chat); seed periodic in-person
meetups; make informal connection a deliberate ritual, not an accident. *Use when* a remote
team feels disconnected.

**Beware the Dragons** — name the real downsides. *Procedure:* watch for cabin fever
(isolation), overwork (people who can't stop), and security hygiene; counter each
explicitly — connection rituals, hard stops/boundaries, basic security discipline. *Use
when* assessing remote risk honestly.

**Hire & Keep the Best** — *Procedure:* hire from everywhere; pay top-of-market regardless
of cost-of-living; prize writing ability; test with a real, short paid project before
committing; manage by output and regular check-ins. *Use when* building or retaining a
remote team.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Reframe to output.** Define the artifact and what "good" looks like — not hours or presence.
2. **Drop the geography/locality filter.** Source the best option wherever it lives.
3. **Set the overlap window.** Decide the minimum synchronous band; default everything else to async + written.
4. **Trust, then verify by results.** Grant autonomy; review the work product, not the activity.
5. **Engineer connection.** Stand up the virtual water cooler; schedule periodic in-person (or shared-context) touchpoints.
6. **Name the dragons.** Put explicit countermeasures on isolation, overwork, and security.
7. **Hire/route for writing and output**, pay/allocate top-of-market, and test with a small real task first.

## In the agentic world order
- **Talent without hubs → models without one vendor.** Route each task to the best model
  wherever it lives; don't lock to one provider's "metro area" out of habit.
- **Stop managing the chairs → judge the artifact.** You can't watch an agent work and it
  wouldn't help. Score the diff, the eval, the passing test — not the token stream.
- **Overlap-then-async → the human-in-the-loop window.** Define a thin synchronous review
  gate; let the rest run async, in writing, with a durable trail. Agents are the ultimate
  async coworkers.
- **Trust as precondition → autonomy by guardrail.** Grant scope by default but bound it
  with permissions and checks; if an agent can't be trusted on a task, narrow the task —
  that's a calibration problem, not a reason to watch every step.
- **Virtual water cooler → shared context/memory.** Agents drift when they run alone; give
  the fleet shared memory, conventions, and a common context store.
- **Beware the dragons → cost and drift.** "Overwork" becomes runaway loops and token burn;
  "isolation" becomes context drift. Build the hard stop and shared memory before scaling.

## Pitfalls
- The authors' own caution: remote's real failure modes are **isolation and overwork**, not
  slacking — watch for people (or loops) that can't stop, and engineer connection deliberately.
- The book is **thin and repetitive** — short essays that raise an issue and skim the fix.
  Treat it as a confidence-builder and checklist, not a complete operating manual; supply the
  depth (performance, conflict, onboarding at scale) it skips.
- It often hand-waves "a tool will solve it" (and the authors **sell those tools** — Basecamp,
  Campfire). Don't mistake buying software for doing the management work.
- Its lens is a **small, high-trust, knowledge-work company.** Don't assume the trust-by-default
  and pay-top-of-market advice transfers unchanged to every org, role, or scale.
