Today's Book
Turn the Ship Around!
by L. David Marquet  ·  2012  ·  Leadership
"Don't move information to authority, move authority to the information."
⏱ 8-min read   ★ 4.2 / 5   272 pages
Read this if… you're the bottleneck — every decision routes through you, human or agent — and you want a concrete, mechanism-by-mechanism playbook for pushing authority down to where the information already is.
01 — Key Takeaways
1.Replace leader-follower with leader-leader. The default org chart manufactures followers who wait for orders. Marquet's alternative grows leaders at every level by handing them genuine decision rights.
2.Push authority to where the information is. The person closest to the problem usually knows the most. Don't drag information up the chain — move the decision down to it.
3.Say "I intend to…". The signature mechanism. Subordinates stop asking permission and start declaring intent; the leader's job shrinks to a nod or a question. Passive followers become active owners.
4.Control is worthless without competence and clarity. Hand over authority too fast and you get chaos. Authority must ride on top of technical competence and a shared picture of the goal.
5.Aim for excellence, don't just avoid errors. An organization run on error-avoidance goes quiet and brittle. Run it on a standard of excellence and people lean in.
6.Change the mechanisms, not the speeches. Culture moved on the Santa Fe because Marquet changed concrete procedures — certify instead of brief, think out loud, deliberate action — not because he gave better motivational talks.
02 — Core Concepts

The Leader-Leader Model — the book's spine. Reject the idea that some give orders and the rest comply; design the organization so everyone exercises leadership where they stand.

Intent-Based Leadership — the operating mode. People announce "I intend to…" rather than await instructions, which forces them to think a level up and lets the leader practice letting go.

Control → Competence → Clarity — the three pillars and their order. Give control, but only as fast as you build the technical competence and the shared clarity of purpose that make control safe.

Deliberate Action & Thinking Out Loud — competence mechanisms. Pause and vocalize intent before acting (cuts errors); narrate your reasoning aloud so the team shares context and can catch mistakes.

Don't Brief, Certify — replace passive briefings with certifications: the team has to prove to its supervisor that it's ready, which shifts ownership of preparation onto the people doing the work.
03 — The Book, Part by Part

Part I — Starting Over. The narrative setup: Marquet takes command of the worst-rated sub in the fleet, realizes the leader-follower playbook is broken, and resolves to achieve excellence rather than merely avoid errors.

Part II — Control. The mechanisms for handing over decision rights: rewrite the genetic code, use "I intend to…", resist the urge to provide solutions, eliminate top-down monitoring, and embrace the inspectors.

Part III — Competence. The skill layer that makes control safe: take deliberate action, learn everywhere, certify don't brief, and specify goals not methods.

Part IV — Clarity. The shared-purpose layer: build trust, articulate guiding principles, use immediate recognition, begin with the end in mind, and encourage a questioning attitude over blind obedience.

05 — The Other Side  ·  Opinions, Criticism & What to Read Next

Why people love it: it's a rare leadership book that's also a gripping true story, and Marquet is honest, humble, and specific — he gives you named mechanisms, not platitudes. Each chapter ends with one concrete practice you can lift directly into your own organization. Stephen Covey called the time aboard Santa Fe the most empowering experience of his career.

Fair criticisms: the ideas aren't wholly original — Marquet openly borrows from Covey's 7 Habits. It's a single case study (one submarine, one all-male late-90s crew) generalized into universal advice, and direct, intent-declaring behavior can land very differently for women or in cultures where it isn't equally rewarded. The chunked, chapter-summary-plus-questions format reads as market-driven self-help to some, and "give control" is easy to quote but hard to do without the competence and clarity scaffolding the book sometimes underweights.

Read next: The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People (Stephen Covey) for the roots Marquet draws on, Multipliers (Liz Wiseman) for the same leaders-grow-leaders thesis, and Team of Teams (Stanley McChrystal) for decentralized command at scale.
⚙ In the Agentic World Order
How this book holds up when AI agents do the work.

Move authority to the information. Your agent already holds the freshest context — the file tree, the test output, the error trace. Routing every decision back through a human approval gate recreates leader-follower. Push the decision down to the agent that has the information, and reserve yourself for intent and review.

"I intend to…" becomes the plan-and-confirm step. Marquet's core mechanic is the agentic-coding pattern in disguise: the agent declares "I intend to refactor X, run Y, then deploy Z," and you nod or redirect. That single move converts a passive command-taker into an active planner — and gives you a cheap checkpoint before action.

Control rides on competence and clarity. Don't grant autonomy faster than you've built it. Competence = evals, tests, and tool access that prove the agent can do the task. Clarity = a system prompt that states the guiding principles and the end goal. Give control only as fast as both are real.

Certify, don't brief; act deliberately. Don't just dump context at the agent — make it certify readiness back to you (restate the plan, list what it will touch). And bake in "deliberate action": have the agent state its intent and reasoning out loud before it writes, so mistakes surface in the plan, not in production.
