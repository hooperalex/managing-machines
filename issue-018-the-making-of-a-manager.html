Today's Book
The Making of a Manager
by Julie Zhuo  ·  2019  ·  Management
"Your job, as a manager, is to get better outcomes from a group of people working together."
⏱ 7-min read   ★ 4.0 / 5   288 pages
Read this if… you just became the boss — of people or of agents — and the ground is shifting under you. This is the warm, practical field guide for the first year, when everyone suddenly looks to you and nobody handed you a manual.
01 — Key Takeaways
1.Management is a multiplier, not a promotion. Your job is to get better outcomes from a group of people working together — by improving its purpose, people, and process. Stop measuring yourself by what you personally ship.
2.The half of you that feels like an impostor is normal. Great managers are made, not born. The first three months are about learning the job in public — name what you don't know rather than performing confidence.
3.Your real job is to help your team succeed, not to be liked or to be the smartest in the room. Trust — built through honesty, caring, and consistency — is the substrate everything else runs on.
4.Feedback is a gift you give early and often. The best feedback is specific, behavior-focused, and frequent — not saved up for a dreaded annual review. Set expectations clearly, then close the loop fast.
5.Hire for the team you want in a year. Hiring is not a chore that interrupts the work — it is the work. Design the role, build a portrait of greatness, and treat every hire as a long-term bet on the team's future.
6.As the team grows, you manage the system, not the tasks. You shift from direct work to setting vision, delegating, and shaping culture — the values and unwritten rules that decide behavior when you're not in the room.
02 — Core Concepts

Purpose, People, Process — Zhuo's master framework. A manager influences outcomes through three levers: purpose (does the team know what success looks like and care about it?), people (do they have the skills and motivation to thrive?), and process (how do they make decisions and work together?).

The Four Paths to Management — nobody starts the same way: the apprentice (your boss splits the team), the pioneer (you founded it), the new boss (you join from outside), and the successor (your manager leaves). Each path has its own first-90-days trap.

The Feedback Model — great feedback is task-specific and frequent, behavior-focused and kindly delivered, and gathered from 360°. Set crystal-clear expectations up front so feedback is a course-correction, not a surprise.

Managing Yourself — you can't lead others past where you've led yourself. Know your strengths and triggers, ask for feedback on your own performance, and beat impostor syndrome by focusing on learning rather than proving.

Nurturing Culture — culture is the set of values and behaviors a team rewards and punishes. As a manager you set it deliberately — through what you celebrate, what you tolerate, and the example you personally model.
03 — The Book, Part by Part

Foundations — What management is & your first three months. Defines the job (purpose, people, process), the four paths in, and how to survive the disorienting transition from maker to manager.

Leading people — Small teams, feedback & managing yourself. The day-to-day craft: building trust, running honest one-on-ones, giving feedback that lands, and handling your own doubts and triggers.

Getting things done — Meetings & making things happen. How to run meetings worth attending, set a clear vision, break big goals into plans, and balance long-term strategy against shipping today.

Scaling — Hiring well, leading a growing team & culture. Hiring as core work, delegating as the team grows beyond your direct reach, and shaping the culture that runs the team when you're not there.
04 — Lines Worth Keeping
"Your role is to improve the purpose, people, and process of your team to get as high a multiplier effect on your collective outcome as you can."
"The first big part of your job as a manager is to ensure that your team knows what success looks like and cares about achieving it."
"This is the crux of management: it is the belief that a team of people can achieve more than a single person going it alone."
05 — The Other Side  ·  Opinions, Criticism & What to Read Next

Why people love it: it's the rare management book aimed squarely at the terrified first-time manager, written with unusual warmth and honesty. Zhuo became a Facebook design VP in her late twenties and writes from the awkward middle of actually learning the job — the impostor feelings, the bad first 1:1s — which makes the advice feel earned, not preached.

Fair criticisms: reviewers find it longer than its insights warrant — dense notes in the first hundred pages, thin thereafter — and some chapters (notably meetings) just restate the basics. It leans soft and psychological early on. It draws almost entirely from one fast-growing big-tech context and never turns a critical eye on Facebook, social media, or power dynamics — including how hard it really is for reports to give a boss honest feedback.

Read next: High Output Management (Andy Grove) for the systems-and-leverage backbone, Radical Candor (Kim Scott) for a deeper feedback toolkit, and The Manager's Path (Camille Fournier) for the engineering-leadership ladder beyond year one.
⚙ In the Agentic World Order
How this book holds up when AI agents do the work.

Purpose, people, process — for a fleet. The same three levers run an agent fleet. Purpose: does each agent have a crisp definition of done and a goal it can't drift from? People: are the agents (their models, tools, context) actually capable of the task? Process: how do runs get reviewed, escalated, and merged? Most fleet failures are a process gap, not a model gap.

Expectations before feedback. Zhuo's feedback rule — set crystal-clear expectations first — is the spec and the eval. An agent can't be "course-corrected" against a standard you never wrote down. Specific, frequent, behavior-focused feedback becomes the eval suite and the prompt diff, not an annual review.

Know what success looks like — and make the agent care. Her first duty of management maps directly: encode "what success looks like" into the system prompt and grading rubric so the agent optimizes for the outcome you actually want, not a proxy it can game.

Culture is the guardrails. Culture is what the team does when you're not watching — for a fleet, that's the values, constraints, and refusal behavior baked into the prompt and tooling. And keep Zhuo's honest warning in view: just as reports hesitate to challenge the boss, an agent will happily tell you it succeeded. Build the 360° check that surfaces the truth.
