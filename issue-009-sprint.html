Today's Book
Sprint
by Jake Knapp  ·  2016  ·  Product / Innovation
"No problem is too large for a sprint."
⏱ 6-min read   ★ 4.1 / 5   288 pages
Read this if… you keep launching things that flop — or you run a fleet of agents — and you want a battle-tested, hour-by-hour process to go from a fuzzy big problem to a tested answer in five days, before you commit real money or quarters of work.
01 — Key Takeaways
1.Compress months into a week. A sprint takes a small team, clears five days, and marches from problem to prototype to real customer test — buying you data before you commit engineering quarters.
2.Start at the end. Before sketching anything, agree on the long-term goal and write down the dangerous assumptions and sprint questions you most need answered.
3.Work alone, together. Skip the brainstorm. Each person sketches solutions individually, then the group decides — this beats loud-voice groupthink and produces deeper ideas.
4.One Decider, real authority. Every sprint needs a single capitalized Decider who owns the call. Without decisive authority, the week dissolves into consensus mush.
5.Fake it — Goldilocks quality. Build a façade prototype with just enough fidelity to provoke honest reactions, then throw it away. Prototypes are disposable; learning is not.
6.Five real customers is enough. Friday's interviews with ~5 target users surface roughly 85% of the big problems. Watching real reactions beats any internal debate.
02 — Core Concepts

The Five-Day Sprint — a fixed week-long arc: Monday map and target, Tuesday sketch, Wednesday decide, Thursday prototype, Friday test. Time-boxed structure is the engine.

Start at the End — open by defining the long-term goal and listing sprint questions and assumptions, so the week aims at the riskiest unknowns, not the easy ones.

Together Alone (the Four-Step Sketch) — generate solutions through structured individual work (notes, ideas, Crazy 8s, a detailed solution sketch) rather than open brainstorming.

The Decider & Structured Decision — a capitalized Decider holds authority; the group converges using Lightning Demos, Art Museum, heat maps, dot voting, and a Rumble, then builds a Storyboard.

Prototype Mindset (Goldilocks Quality) — a realistic façade you can build in a day, just real enough to test. Disposable by design; you optimize for honest reactions, not polish.

Five-Act Interview — Friday's test: five one-on-one customer interviews, watched live by the team, turning a week of guesses into observed evidence.
03 — The Book, Part by Part

Set the Stage. Pick a big-enough challenge, recruit a 7-or-fewer team with a Decider, and clear the time and space (a room, whiteboards, no devices).

Monday — Map & Target. Start at the end, map the problem, ask the experts, and have the Decider choose a single target moment to attack.

Tuesday — Sketch. Lightning Demos to remix existing ideas, then the four-step individual sketch ending in one concrete, anonymous solution per person.

Wednesday — Decide. Critique the sketches (Art Museum, heat maps, dot vote, Rumble), let the Decider choose, and turn the winner into a step-by-step storyboard.

Thursday — Prototype. Adopt a "fake it" mindset and build a realistic, disposable façade by end of day, with divided roles and a stitched-together demo.

Friday — Test & Learn. Run five-act interviews with five target customers while the team watches and takes notes, then find patterns and decide the next move.
04 — Lines Worth Keeping
"No problem is too large for a sprint."
"Goldilocks quality. Create a prototype with just enough quality to evoke honest reactions from customers."
"Lurking beneath every goal are dangerous assumptions. The longer those assumptions remain unexamined, the greater the risk."
05 — The Other Side  ·  Opinions, Criticism & What to Read Next

Why people love it: it's ruthlessly practical — an hour-by-hour playbook with checklists, materials lists, and FAQs, born from 100+ real sprints at Google Ventures. Teams use it to dodge expensive launches and to break analysis-paralysis on hard, fuzzy problems.

Fair criticisms: it can read as one-size-fits-all — critics note the format suits surface and UX questions far more than deep technical or research problems, and it's light on citations and rigor. A five-day test of usability can mistake "people understood it" for "people want it." And the book is nearly silent on what happens after the sprint: it doesn't tell you how to sustain the energy or fold findings into normal org culture.

Read next: Make Time (Knapp & Zeratsky) for the personal-focus sequel, The Lean Startup (Eric Ries) for the build-measure-learn lineage, and Inspired (Marty Cagan) for the broader product-discovery context a sprint plugs into.
⚙ In the Agentic World Order
How this book holds up when AI agents do the work.

The five-day sprint → the agent spike. Knapp's bet — compress months of debate into a time-boxed week of building and testing — is exactly the agent loop. Don't argue about whether a workflow will work; spin up agents to prototype it and put it in front of real cases by Friday. The cycle just compresses from days to hours.

Start at the end → write the eval first. Before you let agents sketch solutions, define the long-term goal and sprint questions as an eval. The riskiest assumption becomes the test the fleet has to pass, not an opinion you defend in review.

Together alone → parallel agents, then decide. Skip the single mega-prompt "brainstorm." Run several agents independently on the same problem (the Crazy 8s of the fleet), then converge — you, the Decider, pick the winner. Diverse parallel drafts beat one consensus output.

Goldilocks prototype → disposable agent builds. Have agents build a façade just real enough to test, and treat it as throwaway. The point is honest signal from real users or data, not a polished system you've grown too attached to kill.

The Decider stays human. A sprint dies without one capitalized decision-maker, and so does an agent fleet. Authority to choose, kill, and ship is the one role you never delegate to the machines.
