Today's Book
The Coming Wave
by Mustafa Suleyman  ·  2023  ·  Technology & Society
"We have to contain the seemingly uncontainable."
⏱ 9-min read   ★ 3.8 / 5   332 pages
Read this if… you deploy the technology this book is warning about — and want the insider's framework for what makes AI and its sibling tech so hard to control, and what "containment" would actually require. Written by DeepMind's and Inflection's co-founder.
01 — Key Takeaways
1.A wave is coming, and it won't stay put. AI and synthetic biology are general-purpose technologies that, like every wave before them, get cheaper, spread fast, and prove almost impossible to stop once demand exists.
2.Containment is the problem of the century. The single test that matters is whether we can monitor, curtail, control, and if needed shut down a technology — and right now, Suleyman argues, we can't.
3.The dilemma has no comfortable exit. Both deploying these tools and refusing them carry catastrophic risk. There is no safe "stop" button; the only route is a narrow path between catastrophe and dystopia.
4.Beware pessimism-aversion. Elites instinctively look away from dark realities because they're uncomfortable. Naming that bias is the precondition for acting on risk instead of wishing it away.
5.Capability, not conversation, is the real bar. Forget chatting Turing tests — the meaningful threshold is an AI that can autonomously achieve a complex real-world goal. Suleyman calls it Artificial Capable Intelligence.
6.Containment needs many locks, not one. No single fix works. Suleyman's ten steps stack technical safety, choke points, audits, treaties, culture, and democratic pressure into a defense-in-depth.
02 — Core Concepts

The Four Features — what makes this wave uniquely hard to contain: asymmetry (small actors wield outsized power), hyper-evolution (innovation at digital speed), omni-use (one tool, countless good and bad uses), and autonomy (systems that act without a human in the loop).

Omni-use — Suleyman's upgrade of "dual-use." These technologies aren't just civilian-or-military; they're general-purpose substrates, "the new electricity," embedded everywhere — which is exactly why you can't cordon them off.

Artificial Capable Intelligence (ACI) — a "Modern Turing Test": not can it talk, but can it autonomously turn a goal into real-world results (his example: make $1M from a $100k stake). Capability is the line that matters.

The Grand Bargain & the Narrow Path — the nation-state trades security and prosperity for our consent to be governed; the wave strains that bargain. Navigating it means threading between two cliffs: openness that invites catastrophe and control that hardens into dystopia.

Containment — the master concept: the ability to monitor, curtail, control, and if necessary close down a technology at any point in its life cycle, through technical, cultural, legal, and political means at once.
03 — The Book, Part by Part

Part I — Homo Technologicus. Why technology proliferates, why containment has historically failed, and a framing of the containment problem itself.

Part II — The Next Wave. The technologies: AI and large models, synthetic biology, robotics, quantum and the wider wave — and the four features (asymmetry, hyper-evolution, omni-use, autonomy) that make them slippery.

Part III — States of Failure. The incentives driving the wave, the fragility of the nation-state, the amplification of risk, and the central dilemma between catastrophe and dystopia.

Part IV — Through the Wave. Containment as the way forward, capped by ten concrete steps — from an "Apollo program" for safety to choke points, audits, treaties, and people power.

05 — The Other Side  ·  Opinions, Criticism & What to Read Next

Why people love it: a #1 New York Times bestseller and a Bill Gates / Yuval Noah Harari favorite, praised as the rare AI-risk book written by someone who actually builds the systems. The "four features" and "containment" vocabulary give a genuinely useful map of why this moment is different.

Fair criticisms: reviewers find it repetitive and high-altitude — long on alarm, short on the nitty-gritty of implementation. The ten steps can read as idealistic wish-lists, counter-arguments and critical-of-industry voices are thin, and there's an obvious tension in a leading AI commercializer calling for restraint. It also leans on a somewhat deterministic view that technology moves in one direction while society merely reacts.

Read next: Superintelligence (Nick Bostrom) for the deep alignment case, The Alignment Problem (Brian Christian) for how control actually fails in practice, and Power and Progress (Acemoglu & Johnson) for the political-economy counterweight on who really steers technology.
⚙ In the Agentic World Order
Suleyman's containment, scaled down to your own agent fleet.

Autonomy is the feature you actually own. Of the four features, asymmetry, hyper-evolution and omni-use are macro forces — but autonomy is the dial on your desk. Containment, fleet-sized, is the ability to monitor, curtail, and kill an agent run. If you can't stop it, you don't run it.

Build your own choke points. Suleyman's macro lever — control the compute, the inputs, the bottlenecks — becomes your permission scopes, API keys, rate limits, and spend caps. Govern agents at the choke point (what they can call), not just by trusting the prompt.

ACI is your acceptance test. The Modern Turing Test — can it autonomously achieve a real goal? — is exactly the bar an agent must clear before you widen its autonomy. Promote agents up the autonomy ladder only against demonstrated capability, not vibes.

Defeat your own pessimism-aversion. The temptation to look away from an agent's failure modes is the trap, scaled down. Red-team the dark scenarios, keep a human kill-switch, and treat containment as defense-in-depth: evals, sandboxes, audits, and shutdown — many locks, not one.
