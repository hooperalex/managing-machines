# Power and Progress

![Cover of Power and Progress by Daron Acemoglu & Simon Johnson](https://covers.openlibrary.org/b/isbn/9781541702530-L.jpg)

**by Daron Acemoglu & Simon Johnson · 2023 · Economics / Technology**

> "We are told that the path of technology cannot and should not be obstructed… But this is one of the most pernicious ideas of all."

⏱ 9-min read · ★ 4.0 / 5 · 560 pages

**Read this if…** you design or deploy automation and refuse to treat "the technology decided it" as an answer. This is the case that the direction of progress is a choice — and that someone is making it.

---

## 01 — Key Takeaways

1. **Progress is not automatic.** Technology raises output, but who captures the gains is decided by power and institutions — not by the machine. Shared prosperity is the exception in history, won by countervailing pressure, not a default.
2. **The productivity bandwagon is conditional.** The comforting story — more productivity automatically means more jobs and higher wages — only holds when technology creates new tasks for workers and labor has the power to bargain. Neither is guaranteed.
3. **Beware "so-so automation."** Tech that replaces a worker while barely beating them adds little to total output — it just shifts income from labor to capital. It is the worst of both worlds and depressingly common.
4. **Direction beats acceleration.** The useful question isn't "how fast?" but "which way?" Technology can *automate* humans out or *augment* what they can do. That fork is a design choice, repeated daily.
5. **Vision is power.** A narrow "vision oligarchy" of technologists and financiers decides what counts as progress and persuades everyone else to go along. Controlling the agenda is as decisive as controlling the capital.
6. **Redirection is possible — and political.** History shows technology bent toward broad benefit only when unions, regulation, taxes, and counter-visions pushed back. The same levers apply to AI now.

---

## 02 — Core Concepts

**The Productivity Bandwagon** — the claim that rising productivity automatically lifts wages and employment. It works only when new tasks raise labor's marginal product *and* workers have bargaining power; absent either, the bandwagon stalls.

**Automation vs. New Tasks** — the central fork. Automation substitutes machines for workers and shifts income to capital; new-task (augmenting) technology raises what workers can do and pulls wages up. The mix determines who wins.

**So-So Automation** — automation that is only marginally better than the human it replaces. It generates big distributional losses for workers and small productivity gains for society — the worst trade-off, yet often chosen because it cuts the wage bill.

**Machine Usefulness (vs. Machine Intelligence)** — the authors' alternative agenda: build tools that make humans more capable and informed, not autonomous systems that imitate and replace them. Complement people; don't mimic them.

**Power, Vision & the Vision Oligarchy** — power is the ability to achieve your objectives, including through persuasion. A small elite shapes the dominant vision of "progress," steering investment and silencing alternatives. Redirecting tech means contesting that vision.

---

## 03 — The Book, Part by Part

**Vision & Power (Ch. 1–3).** The book's engine: how a ruling vision directs technology, why progress isn't automatic, and how the productivity bandwagon really works (and fails).

**A Thousand Years of History (Ch. 4–6).** Medieval cathedrals, the agricultural revolution, the Industrial Revolution, and the Suez Canal — case studies where elites captured the gains until counter-power forced a redistribution.

**The Brief Shared Century (Ch. 7).** The post-war decades when unions, regulation, and augmenting technology spread gains widely — the authors' proof that broad prosperity is achievable, not utopian.

**The Digital Turn (Ch. 8–9).** How computing tilted toward automation and surveillance, hollowed the middle, and concentrated wealth — plus AI and the road to dystopian "so-so" futures.

**Redirecting Technology (Ch. 10–11).** The remedy: rebuild countervailing power and reform incentives — taxes, data rights, antitrust, worker voice, and a pro-human R&D agenda for "machine usefulness."

## 05 — The Other Side · Opinions, Criticism & What to Read Next

**Why people love it:** a sweeping, learned rebuttal to tech determinism from two of the field's heaviest hitters (Acemoglu is a 2024 Nobel laureate in economics). The "automate vs. augment" framing and "so-so automation" gave the AI-and-labor debate a sharp, usable vocabulary, and the history is genuinely engrossing.

**Fair criticisms:** reviewers (notably Noah Smith) charge cherry-picked history — e.g., dismissing the Haber-Bosch process that feeds billions — and a bibliographic essay instead of footnotes that makes claims hard to check. Defining "power" to include persuasion risks tautology ("they won because they had power"). And the remedy — experts steering a "menu" of technologies — is vague and assumes foresight history rarely rewards. The book is long and, at 560 pages, repetitive.

**Read next:** *Why Nations Fail* (Acemoglu & Robinson) for the institutions thesis underneath this one, *The Second Machine Age* (Brynjolfsson & McAfee) for the optimist's counterpoint, and *Race Against the Machine* for the short version of the automation debate.

## ⚙ In the Agentic World Order

*How this book holds up when AI agents do the work.*

**Automate vs. augment is your weekly design choice.** Every agent you ship sits on Acemoglu & Johnson's fork: replace a person's task wholesale, or build a tool that makes them sharper and faster. "Machine usefulness" says default to augmentation — agents that brief, draft, and check humans — and reserve full automation for tasks where it genuinely clears the bar.

**Hunt down your "so-so" agents.** The book's sharpest warning for operators: an agent that replaces a worker while barely beating them destroys value — you eat oversight, error, and morale costs for a tiny output gain. Measure end-to-end quality, not headcount removed. If the agent is only "so-so," keep the human and augment instead.

**Watch the productivity bandwagon in your own org.** Deploying agents doesn't automatically lift your team — gains only spread if you reinvest freed time into new, higher-value tasks and share the upside. Otherwise the surplus pools at the top and your best people leave. The redistribution is a management decision.

**Don't outsource your vision to the vision oligarchy.** The frontier-lab roadmap is one vision of progress, optimized for their objectives, not yours. Keep a counter-vision: decide deliberately which tasks stay human, instrument who captures the gains, and treat "the model can do it" as the start of the argument, not the end.
