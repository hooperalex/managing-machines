# Atomic Habits — James Clear's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Atomic Habits* (James Clear, 2018). This skill lets you **apply** Clear's system,
not just describe it. Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 001.

## When to use
- The user wants to start, build, break, or stick with a habit.
- A goal keeps failing and willpower/motivation is being blamed.
- The user wants a repeatable *system* for consistent behavior.
- The user is designing routines for themselves OR operating habits for managing agents.

## Core operating principles
1. **Systems over goals.** "You do not rise to the level of your goals; you fall to the
   level of your systems." Design the daily system, not the outcome.
2. **Identity first.** Three layers: outcomes → processes → **identity**. Lasting change
   starts at identity: "Become the type of person who…". Every action is a vote for that
   identity. Ask: *"Who is the kind of person that would solve this?"*
3. **1% compounding.** Small improvements compound; results lag effort (the "Plateau of
   Latent Potential"). Don't quit in the valley before the breakthrough.

## The frameworks

**The Habit Loop** — every habit = *cue → craving → response → reward*. Diagnose where a
habit is breaking by which beat is missing.

**The Four Laws (to BUILD a habit)** — map onto the loop:
1. **Make it obvious** (cue): use *implementation intentions* — "I will [behavior] at
   [time] in [location]"; *habit stacking* — "After [current habit], I will [new habit]";
   design the environment so the cue is unmissable.
2. **Make it attractive** (craving): *temptation bundling* — pair a want with a should;
   join a culture where the behavior is normal.
3. **Make it easy** (response): reduce friction; the **Two-Minute Rule** — scale the habit
   down to a two-minute on-ramp ("read one page"); master showing up before optimizing.
4. **Make it satisfying** (reward): add an immediate reward; **habit tracking** ("don't
   break the chain"); the rule of **never miss twice**.

**The inversion (to BREAK a habit):** make it invisible, unattractive, difficult,
unsatisfying.

**The Goldilocks Rule** — sustained motivation comes from working on tasks of
*just-manageable* difficulty.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Reframe to identity.** Restate the goal as an identity ("I am someone who ships daily").
2. **Pick the direction.** Building a habit → use the Four Laws. Breaking one → use the
   inversion.
3. **Find the broken beat.** Walk the habit loop; locate the weakest of cue/craving/
   response/reward.
4. **Apply the matching law(s)**, concretely. Write the implementation intention and/or
   habit stack verbatim. Shrink the first action to two minutes.
5. **Make it stick.** Add a tracker and the "never miss twice" rule.
6. **Lower the bar until adherence is ~100%**, then scale (Goldilocks).

## In the agentic world order
Apply the same system to *operating habits* for managing AI agents:
- **Systems over agents.** Your output falls to the level of the agents you've configured
  and the guardrails around them. Build the system, not one-off prompts.
- **Identity → operator.** Cultivate the identity "I delegate clearly and verify the
  result." The keystone habit becomes *reviewing agent output*, daily.
- **Two-Minute Rule, inverted.** The friction is no longer starting the task — it's
  starting to delegate. Make the first delegation a two-minute, one-instruction handoff.
- **Habit tracking → run logs.** "Never miss twice" becomes "never ship an unreviewed run
  twice." Compound 1% gains into prompts, tools, and review loops.
- **Guard against atrophy.** When agents do the reps, deliberately keep the human skills
  the reps used to build.

## Pitfalls
- Clear's science isn't novel — it repackages Duhigg and BJ Fogg; treat it as a practical
  system, not new research.
- Watch for survivorship bias. The book's hero anecdote (British Cycling's "1% marginal
  gains") credits habits for results that also came from huge funding, elite coaches, and
  better equipment. Don't attribute outsized outcomes to habit tweaks alone.
- It's anecdote-heavy and assumes a fairly stable life; it says little about habit change
  under addiction, poverty, or clinical conditions. Don't over-apply it there.
- Don't chase the perfect plan — adherence beats optimization. Showing up wins.
