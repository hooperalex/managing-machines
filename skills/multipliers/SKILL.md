# Multipliers — Liz Wiseman's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Multipliers: How the Best Leaders Make Everyone Smarter* (Liz Wiseman, 2010;
revised 2017). This skill lets you **apply** Wiseman's model, not just describe it.
Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 008.

## When to use
- The user leads a team (or a fleet of agents) and wants more usable capability from it.
- A delegation / autonomy question: how much to hand off vs. step in.
- The team has gone quiet, passive, or dependent on the leader for every answer.
- The user is a capable, helpful leader who suspects they may be capping their people.
- Designing decision processes, stretch assignments, or ownership/accountability.

## Core principle
**Leaders fall on a spectrum from Diminisher to Multiplier.** Diminishers are absorbed in
their own intelligence and shut down the smarts around them; Multipliers are *genius makers*
who access and grow the intelligence of others — getting roughly **2x** more capability from
the same people. The difference is a **mindset**: Diminishers assume people can't figure it
out without them; Multipliers assume people are smart and will. The job is to build the
conditions where that intelligence gets used.

## The frameworks

**The Talent Magnet** — attract talent and use it at its highest point of contribution.
*Procedure:* find each person's native genius (what they do easily and freely); assign work
to that; remove the blockers (often yourself); broadcast their contribution so they grow a
reputation. *Use when* people are underused or talent keeps leaving. Contrast: the Empire
Builder who hoards people and lets capability sit idle.

**The Liberator** — create intense pressure for people's *best thinking*, not compliance.
*Procedure:* give real room to think *and* demand excellence; release your own airtime so
others fill it; make it safe to make mistakes but mandatory to learn from them. *Use when*
the room has gone quiet or only your ideas get aired. Contrast: the Tyrant whose anxiety
shrinks everyone's thinking.

**The Challenger** — seed a concrete, daunting challenge and let the team own the path.
*Procedure:* frame a hard, specific opportunity; ask the questions that expose the gap;
*withhold the answer* so the team has to reach; let them build belief by figuring it out.
*Use when* you want growth and stretch, not just task completion. Contrast: the Know-It-All
who hands down directives.

**The Debate Maker** — drive sound decisions through rigorous debate. *Procedure:* frame
the question and the stakes up front; gather the evidence and the right people; run a real
debate (assign positions, demand reasons); then decide clearly. The team gets a better call
*and* is ready to execute it. *Use when* decisions are contested, high-stakes, or made too
narrowly. Contrast: the Decision Maker who decides in a small inner circle.

**The Investor** — give ownership of results, invest in success, then hold full
accountability. *Procedure:* name the owner; give them the resources, coaching, and stake;
when they bring you a problem, hand it *back* with support rather than solving it; let
natural consequences land. *Use when* people keep escalating decisions to you. Contrast: the
Micromanager who jumps in and out and owns nothing fully.

**The Accidental Diminisher** — the book's signature diagnostic: capable, well-meaning
leaders who diminish by *over*using a strength. *Procedure:* check yourself against the
common types — the Idea Guy (floods the room so others stop thinking), the Always-On
energizer (sucks the oxygen), the Rescuer (jumps in and creates dependence), the Pacesetter
(moves so fast others can't keep up), the Optimist (dismisses the hard parts), the
Perfectionist. *Use when* you're trying to help but the team has gone passive.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Check your mindset.** Do you actually believe this person/agent is smart and capable?
   If not, you'll diminish no matter the technique. Lead with the assumption of intelligence.
2. **Diagnose accidental diminishing.** Which of your strengths might be capping the team?
   Name it before reaching for a discipline.
3. **Be the Talent Magnet.** Match each person to their highest point of contribution; clear
   the blockers (often your own involvement).
4. **Liberate, then challenge.** Make room and demand best thinking; then plant a concrete
   stretch challenge and *withhold the answer*.
5. **Make decisions by debate**, not decree — frame the question, gather evidence, run it,
   decide cleanly.
6. **Invest and give it back.** Hand ownership and accountability over; when problems return
   to you, return them with support instead of solving them.
7. **Measure capability used, honestly.** Watch whether people are getting *smarter* and more
   independent over time — not whether they're busy or compliant.

## In the agentic world order
- **Mindset = your prior on the model.** Treating a capable agent as if it can't reason
  (over-specifying every step) caps it at your imagination. The Multiplier states the goal
  and the bar, then lets the model reason — the right prior for a strong model.
- **Accidental Diminisher = the hand-holding operator.** Pre-chewing every plan, babysitting
  each prompt, and rescuing on the first stumble trains *learned helplessness*: pipelines
  that can't run without you. The Rescuer and Pacesetter anti-patterns map directly to
  brittle, human-in-every-loop agent workflows.
- **Talent Magnet → route to native genius.** Send each task to the model/agent best suited
  to it (cheap/fast vs. deep-reasoning vs. tool-use), at its highest point of contribution.
- **Challenger → goal + criteria, not steps.** Give a concrete, stretching objective and
  clear success criteria; withhold the step-by-step. Reserve detailed direction for genuinely
  low-maturity tasks.
- **Debate Maker → multi-agent debate.** Run critic/proposer or debate loops to pressure-test
  decisions before committing — the discipline implemented as architecture.
- **Investor → ownership + evals.** Give the agent a whole task with the tools and context to
  succeed, and enforce accountability through evals and review gates — instead of
  micromanaging it in and out of every subtask.

## Pitfalls
- **Wiseman's own caution:** most diminishing is *accidental* and done by your best
  intentions. The technique won't help if the underlying mindset is "I'm the smartest here."
  Fix the assumption first.
- **Don't swing to absent.** Multiplier is *not* hands-off; it's demanding *and* liberating.
  Withholding the answer without giving room, support, or a clear challenge is just neglect.
- **The "2x" is self-reported.** The headline capability gain rests on perceived capability
  used, not measured output. Treat it as a vivid frame, not a precise metric — and verify
  with real outcomes.
- **The ideas aren't new, and the model is generic.** Empowering leadership has circulated
  for decades; the framework is deliberately industry-agnostic. It's a useful vocabulary, not
  a complete operating system — pair it with the culture, trust, and psychological-safety
  scaffolding (e.g., Lencioni, Edmondson) that makes any model actually stick.
