# Team of Teams — Stanley McChrystal's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Team of Teams: New Rules of Engagement for a Complex World* (Stanley McChrystal
with Tantum Collins, David Silverman & Chris Fussell, 2015). This skill lets you **apply**
McChrystal's operating system, not just describe it. Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 004.

## When to use
- A capable org (or agent fleet) is losing to a faster, less-resourced rival or a problem
  that keeps mutating.
- Silos slow you down: teams optimize locally and the whole moves slowly.
- A decentralization / autonomy question: how much authority to push down, and to whom.
- Designing information flow, transparency, or a shared-context layer across teams/agents.
- Diagnosing whether your environment is *complicated* (optimize) or *complex* (adapt).

## Core principle
**In a complex world, adaptability beats efficiency.** You cannot predict the next shock, so
stop engineering one brilliant, brittle solution and instead build an organization that can
sense and respond. Scale the *qualities* of a small team — trust, shared purpose, shared
awareness — across many teams, then push decisions to the people (or agents) closest to the
problem.

## The frameworks

**Complicated vs. Complex** — diagnose the environment first. *Procedure:* ask whether
outcomes are predictable from inputs. Predictable but intricate (a clock) = complicated →
optimize and standardize. Dense interdependencies, surprising behavior (a market, an
insurgency, a live codebase) = complex → favor resilience and adaptation. *Use when*
choosing between "tune the machine" and "build for change."

**Resilience over Optimization** — engineer to absorb disruption, not to peak. *Procedure:*
identify single points of failure; add redundancy, slack, and graceful degradation; accept
some inefficiency as the price of surviving the unexpected. *Use when* the environment is
complex and shocks are unpredictable.

**Team of Teams (scale the small-team magic)** — *Procedure:* take what makes one small team
fast (trust + common purpose + shared awareness) and replicate the *connections* across
teams rather than growing one big team. Build bonds between teams, not just within them.
*Use when* the org has grown and lost the speed it had when it was small.

**Shared Consciousness** — the prerequisite for everything else. *Procedure:* (1) create
*extreme participatory transparency* — a regular all-hands "O&I"-style forum and open
information so everyone sees the whole system in near-real time; (2) build *lateral
connectivity* — embed/liaise people across teams so trust crosses silos. Shift the norm from
"need to know" to "need to share." *Use when* teams act on partial pictures or distrust each
other.

**Empowered Execution** — decentralized decision-making. *Procedure:* once shared context
exists, push authority down to those closest to the problem; the leader sets intent and
removes themselves from the approval loop for all but the highest-stakes calls. *Use when*
the top has become the bottleneck and tempo is too slow.

**Leading Like a Gardener (Eyes-On, Hands-Off)** — *Procedure:* spend your leadership energy
cultivating the ecosystem — culture, context, information flow, trust boundaries — not
directing individual moves. Stay *informed* (Eyes-On) while staying *out of the execution*
(Hands-Off). *Use when* you're tempted to control each decision yourself.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Diagnose the environment.** Complicated or complex? If complex, stop optimizing for
   efficiency and design for adaptability.
2. **Set and broadcast intent.** Write the mission and the few non-negotiables, so empowered
   people make aligned choices. (Don't skip this — autonomy without strategy floods you with
   fast motion in the wrong direction.)
3. **Build shared consciousness.** Stand up a recurring transparency forum + a shared
   information surface; add lateral links between the teams that must cooperate.
4. **Push decisions down.** Grant bounded authority to those closest to the problem; reserve
   sign-off for irreversible, high-blast-radius moves only.
5. **Lead like a gardener.** Tend the environment (culture, tools, guardrails); resist
   re-inserting yourself into execution.
6. **Build resilience in.** Add redundancy and graceful degradation so the system survives
   the shock you didn't predict.

## In the agentic world order
- **Your environment is complex, not complicated.** A fleet faces an environment that changes
  every commit/model/API. Don't over-engineer one brittle pipeline — build for resilience:
  retries, fallbacks, graceful degradation so the swarm keeps functioning when something moves.
- **Shared consciousness → shared context.** The O&I forum becomes a shared scratchpad,
  memory store, or message bus every agent reads. "Need to share," not "need to know," so
  specialized agents stay coherent without a central router relaying every fact.
- **Empowered execution → real autonomy.** If you approve every tool call, you *are* the
  bottleneck. Grant agents bounded authority to act on the context they hold; reserve human
  sign-off for the few irreversible, high-blast-radius moves — not every raid.
- **Gardener → ecosystem design.** You don't direct each agent's move; you cultivate the
  system prompts, shared tools, guardrails, and trust boundaries. Eyes-On = observability,
  traces, evals. Hands-Off = let them execute.
- **Set intent before letting go.** Decentralized agents amplify whatever direction they're
  pointed in. Encode the mission and non-negotiables first.

## Pitfalls
- **Author's caution — strategy still matters.** McChrystal's reviewers flag that the book is
  light on strategy: empowered execution without a clear strategy/intent produces fast,
  confident motion in the wrong direction. Set direction before decentralizing.
- **Empowered execution presumes high trust + competence.** The Task Force was elite. Most
  orgs (and unproven agents) aren't — build the trust, context, and skill first, or
  decentralization just spreads mistakes faster.
- **Transparency has costs.** Extreme transparency consumes attention and can leak sensitive
  context; scope what's shared and to whom (the same applies to shared agent memory/state).
- **The context doesn't transfer cleanly.** Special-ops lessons don't map 1:1 to a sales team,
  a hospital, or a CI pipeline. Adapt the principles; don't cargo-cult the war stories.
- **The book is repetitive and the principles loosely defined.** Extract the operating
  procedure (above) rather than re-reading the "world is complex" argument five times.
