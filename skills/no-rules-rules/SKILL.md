# No Rules Rules — Hastings & Meyer's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *No Rules Rules: Netflix and the Culture of Reinvention* (Reed Hastings & Erin
Meyer, 2020). This skill lets you **apply** the Netflix operating system, not just describe
it. Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 022.

## When to use
- The user wants to remove process, approvals, or controls and run on judgment + speed.
- A "how much freedom can this team/agent handle?" question.
- Designing feedback culture, performance bars, or who to keep vs let go.
- Deciding how to delegate a decision without a top-down sign-off.
- The agentic equivalent: setting autonomy, evals, and guardrails for a fleet.

## Core principle
**Freedom and Responsibility reinforce each other — but only in the right order.** You earn
the right to remove a control by first raising **talent density** and then **candor**. Skip
the order and freedom produces chaos. So the job is: get the capability high, make feedback
relentless and safe, *then* delete the rules.

## The frameworks

**Freedom & Responsibility (F&R)** — the operating system. *What:* more freedom + more
responsibility, not a trade-off. *Procedure:* for any control you want to cut, confirm the
team can carry the matching responsibility first; if yes, remove the rule and hold people
accountable for the outcome. *Use when* deciding whether a policy/approval should exist.

**Talent Density** — the share of top performers. *What:* the prerequisite for everything
else. *Procedure:* raise the bar on every hire; treat one weak performer as a tax on
everyone around them; do not loosen controls until density is high. *Use when* the team
isn't ready for the freedom you want to give.

**The 4A Feedback Model** — makes candor routine and safe. *What:* two A's for giving (Aim
to assist, Actionable) and two for receiving (Appreciate, Accept or discard). *Procedure:*
require specific, helpful, actionable feedback; the receiver thanks and decides — they own
whether to act. *Use when* installing a feedback culture or running reviews.

**The Keeper Test (no brilliant jerks)** — the performance bar. *What:* keep only people
you'd fight to retain. *Procedure:* per person ask "if they tried to leave for a similar
role, would I fight to keep them?" If no, offer a generous severance now rather than a PIP.
Reject brilliant jerks — the cost to teamwork outweighs the talent. *Use when* the bar is
slipping or someone is "adequate."

**Top-of-Market Pay** — fund density. *What:* pay personal top of market, favor high salary
over performance bonuses. *Procedure:* benchmark each person to their market high; raise
proactively; skip bonus games that distort focus. *Use when* you're trying to attract/keep
the best.

**Lead with Context, not Control** — replace approval with alignment. *What:* set the
context (strategy, goals, stakes, assumptions) so people decide well themselves.
*Procedure:* before delegating, transmit the context not the decision; name the **Informed
Captain** who owns the call and the bet; judge by outcomes over a set of decisions, not by
asking permission. *Use when* you're tempted to add a sign-off step. Requires high density,
an innovation (not error-prevention) goal, and a loosely coupled system.

**Farm for Dissent & Sunshine Mistakes** — de-risk big calls and make risk-taking safe.
*What:* actively seek disagreement before deciding; openly share your own failures.
*Procedure:* for a meaningful bet, solicit written dissent / socialize the idea and tally
disagreement before committing; when a bet fails, "sunshine" it publicly so others keep
taking smart risks. *Use when* a decision is big, irreversible, or groupthink-prone.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Check density first.** Is the team/agent genuinely high-capability on THIS work? If
   not, raise density before removing any control.
2. **Turn up candor.** Install the 4A loop so feedback is frequent, specific, and safe.
3. **Remove one control.** Pick a rule that's friction; delete it and hold people
   responsible for the outcome instead.
4. **Lead with context.** Transmit strategy + stakes; name the Informed Captain; drop the
   approval gate.
5. **Farm for dissent** on the big bet; **sunshine** it if it fails.
6. **Run the Keeper Test** on a cadence; keep only those you'd fight for, with a generous
   exit for the rest.

## In the agentic world order
- **Talent density = model & harness quality.** Don't loosen guardrails on a weak model. A
  failing run on a hard task is a density problem — upgrade model, tools, and context first.
- **Candor → evals & the 4A loop.** Evals, critics, and self-review prompts are the agent's
  4A: aim-to-assist, actionable feedback fed straight into the next run.
- **Lead with context → system prompt.** Encode strategy, constraints, and stakes in the
  prompt; let the agent be the **Informed Captain** instead of gating every action.
- **Farm for Dissent → adversarial critic.** Run a red-team / critic pass before committing
  to a plan; tally the objections, then decide.
- **Keeper Test → prune the fleet.** Cut prompts, configs, and sub-agents you wouldn't fight
  to keep; don't nurse an "adequate" agent that quietly drifts.
- **Remove controls last.** Freedom for agents is gated by instrumentation — only delete an
  approval step once evals reliably catch the failures it was guarding against.

## Pitfalls
- **Order matters (author's own caution):** removing controls before density and candor are
  high produces chaos, not freedom. Never skip the sequence.
- **Survivorship bias:** the model is told by the people who stayed, from Netflix's own
  self-report. Don't assume it transfers to lower-margin, tightly-coupled, or
  error-intolerant work — and for agents, remember they can't candidly self-report failure,
  so external evals are non-negotiable.
- **The Keeper Test can become fear.** "Adequate gets a severance" risks an anxiety-driven,
  fire-fast culture; pair it with genuine candor and psychological safety, not just threat.
- **Context-leading needs preconditions.** It requires high talent density, an innovation
  goal, and loose coupling. In high-stakes, tightly-coupled, or compliance-bound work, keep
  the control.
- **Cross-cultural friction:** candor and freedom read very differently across cultures
  (Meyer's chapter) — calibrate directness and hierarchy to context rather than copying
  Netflix wholesale.
