# Essentialism — Greg McKeown's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Essentialism: The Disciplined Pursuit of Less* (Greg McKeown, 2014). This skill lets
you **apply** McKeown's discipline of "less but better," not just describe it. Extracted by
Managing Machines, Issue 030.

## When to use
- The user (or their agent fleet) is spread thin and making millimeters of progress on everything.
- A prioritization or "what should I say no to?" decision.
- Pruning a backlog, a roadmap, a toolset, or an agent's permitted actions.
- Escaping a sunk-cost project or a runaway agent loop.
- Designing a workflow/pipeline for focus, buffers, and steady small wins.

## Core principle
**"If you don't prioritize your life, someone else will."** Almost everything is noise; a vital
few efforts produce nearly all the value. The job is not to do more efficiently — it is to do
*less, but better*, by relentlessly filtering for what is essential and discarding the rest.

## The frameworks

**Essentialist vs. Nonessentialist mindset** — the lens you operate from. *Procedure:* catch the
Nonessentialist defaults ("I have to," "it's all important," "I can fit it all in") and swap them
for Essentialist ones ("I choose to," "only a few things matter," "what's the trade-off?").
*Use when* you feel obligated, scattered, or unable to refuse.

**The Power of Choice & Trade-offs** — choice is an ability you exercise, not a thing that
happens to you. *Procedure:* name the trade-off explicitly — ask "which problem do I want?"
rather than pretending you can have it all. *Use when* you're avoiding a decision or saying yes
by default.

**The 90% Rule (extreme criteria)** — a selection filter. *Procedure:* define the single most
important criterion; score each option 0–100 against it; anything below 90 counts as a zero and
is rejected. *Use when* "pretty good" options are crowding out the essential ones.

**Explore** — search wide before committing narrow. *Procedure:* create space to think (escape),
look at what actually matters (signal vs. noise), play, protect sleep/recovery, then select with
extreme criteria. *Use when* you're committing before you've seen the real options.

**Eliminate** — cut the trivial many. *Procedure:* clarify one essential intent that pre-decides
a thousand later choices; dare to say a graceful **no**; **uncommit** from sunk costs (the past
spend is gone — judge only future value); edit aggressively; set protective boundaries. *Use
when* your plate is full of good-but-not-essential commitments.

**Execute** — make the essential effortless. *Procedure:* build **buffers** (slack for the
unexpected); find and remove the **"slowest hiker"** constraint throttling everything; aim for
**smallest viable progress** (small, real wins); routinize the right behavior so it becomes the
path of least resistance; stay focused on what matters now. *Use when* good intentions keep
dying in execution.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Reframe to less-but-better.** Restate the goal as the vital few outcomes, not the full list.
2. **Name the criterion.** Pick the one filter that matters most for this decision.
3. **Explore, then select with the 90% Rule.** See the options; reject everything below 90.
4. **Eliminate.** For each survivor that still isn't essential, say a graceful no or uncommit —
   ignore sunk cost; judge only forward value.
5. **Design the execution.** Add buffers, remove the single biggest constraint, define the
   smallest viable progress, and routinize it.
6. **Re-run on a cadence.** Essentialism is a constant discipline, not a one-time purge.

## In the agentic world order
- **Vital few, not infinite many.** Cheap agents tempt you to run everything; agent capacity is
  abundant but *your* review/trust/integration attention is the scarce bottleneck. Point the
  fleet only at the workstreams that move the goal.
- **90% Rule on the backlog and the toolbox.** Score each candidate agent task — and each tool
  you expose — against one criterion; below 90 is a zero. A lean toolset beats a sprawling agent
  that could do anything and reliably does nothing essential.
- **No as a guardrail; uncommit as kill-switch.** Bake scope refusal into the system prompt
  (refusal beats scope creep). Kill a multi-step loop burning tokens toward a dead end — sunk
  budget is irrelevant; judge only expected forward value.
- **Execute by design.** Give agents token/retry **buffers**, remove the one "slowest hiker"
  step throttling the pipeline, and design for **smallest viable progress** — narrow, verifiable
  increments — so the fleet ships steadily instead of sprinting into unreviewable sprawl.

## Pitfalls
- McKeown's own caution: the discipline is constant, not a single cleanup — and an essential
  intent must be both concrete and inspirational, or "clarify one decision" degenerates into a
  vague mission statement that pre-decides nothing.
- **Privilege blind spot.** The "eliminate the non-essential" advice assumes you can. Caregivers,
  frontline workers, and early-stage founders have non-negotiable demands; apply the filter to
  what's genuinely optional, not to obligations.
- **The binary is too clean.** "Essentialist vs. Nonessentialist" flattens a messy reality; many
  situations are not one clear essential-vs-trivial choice. Use it as a prompt, not a verdict.
- **Repetition ≠ depth.** The core idea is small; don't mistake re-stating "less but better" for
  having done the hard work of actually choosing and cutting.
- Don't let "be present now" override "think long-term" — McKeown gives both; keep the horizon
  for direction and presence for execution, rather than swapping one for the other.
