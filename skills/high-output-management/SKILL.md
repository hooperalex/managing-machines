# High Output Management — Andy Grove's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *High Output Management* (Andrew S. Grove, 1983). This skill lets you **apply**
Grove's operating system, not just describe it. Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 002.

## When to use
- The user manages a team (or a fleet of agents) and wants to raise output.
- A delegation / autonomy question: how much to hand off, how closely to supervise.
- Designing metrics, dashboards, or quality gates.
- Structuring 1:1s, meetings, or objective-setting (OKRs).
- Diagnosing a bottleneck in a workflow or pipeline.

## Core principle
**A manager's output = the output of their organization + the output of the neighboring
units they influence.** You are measured by what your people (or agents) produce, not by
your own activity. So the job is to maximize the output of the system around you.

## The frameworks

**Managerial Leverage** — productivity = output ÷ time, and every activity has a leverage
multiple. *Procedure:* list your activities; estimate how many people/runs each one
affects; shift time toward the high-leverage few (training, clarifying intent, removing a
bottleneck, deciding at the right moment). *Use when* you feel busy but low-impact.

**Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM)** — competence at a *specific* task (not a person's general
seniority). *Procedure:* rate the person/agent's maturity on THIS task → low: give
structure, tight instructions, frequent check-ins; medium: emphasize communication and
support; high: delegate and monitor by results. *Use when* deciding how hands-on to be.
There is no best style — only the one that fits the TRM.

**One-on-Ones** — the highest-ROI manager tool; mentoring + information flow. *Procedure:*
schedule recurring 1:1s; the subordinate owns the agenda; cadence scales inversely with
TRM (lower maturity → more frequent). *Use when* output or information flow is degrading.

**Management By Objectives (OKRs)** — answer two questions: "Where do I want to go?"
(objective) and "How will I know I'm getting there?" (a few measurable key results).
*Procedure:* set one clear objective + 2–4 key results on a short horizon; review against
them. *Use when* aligning effort or planning.

**The Production / Limiting-Step Principle** — treat work as a flow. *Procedure:* find the
limiting (bottleneck) step and schedule it first / protect it; do the longest-lead-time or
costliest step earliest; inspect quality at the lowest-value-added stage (cheap, early).
*Use when* a pipeline is slow or quality is failing late.

**Indicators & the Black Box** — you can't see inside the work, so instrument it.
*Procedure:* pick *paired* indicators so one can't be gamed at the other's expense
(quantity + quality), add leading indicators, and watch trends not single points. *Use
when* you need visibility or are setting metrics.

**The Three Modes of Control** — behavior is governed by (1) free-market forces,
(2) contractual obligations, or (3) cultural values. *Procedure:* gauge the
Complexity / Uncertainty / Ambiguity (CUA) of the situation — low CUA can run on
contracts/metrics; high CUA needs shared cultural values. *Use when* rules keep failing to
produce the behavior you want.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Reframe to output.** Restate the goal as the output of the team/system, not your tasks.
2. **Find the leverage.** What few activities would lift the whole system's output most?
3. **Set direction.** Write one objective + 2–4 key results.
4. **Calibrate per person/agent.** Judge TRM on the task; pick structure vs delegation.
5. **Instrument it.** Choose paired indicators; place a cheap early quality check.
6. **Pick the control mode** by CUA; if ambiguity is high, lead with shared values, not rules.
7. **Run the 1:1 loop** to keep information and coaching flowing; adjust cadence to TRM.

## In the agentic world order
- **Output = your fleet's output.** Judge yourself by what the agents produce. Optimize the
  system, not your keystrokes.
- **Highest-leverage acts** are now the system prompt, the eval, and the review gate — each
  multiplies across thousands of runs. Improving prompts/tools/context = "training the fleet."
- **TRM → agent autonomy.** Unproven agent on a novel task → tight structure + heavy review
  (low TRM). Proven agent on a known task → delegate + monitor by results (high TRM).
- **1:1 → review cadence.** Frequency of human review scales inversely with the agent's
  demonstrated maturity on the task.
- **Black box → dashboards/evals.** Use paired indicators (quantity *and* quality) and cheap
  early checks at the lowest-value stage.
- **Modes of control → guardrails vs principles.** High complexity/ambiguity can't be ruled
  by hard constraints alone — govern agents with internalized "cultural values" (the
  principles in the system prompt).

## Pitfalls
- Grove's own caution: pick indicators in pairs so optimizing one doesn't wreck another
  (a measured thing gets gamed — Goodhart's law).
- The book over-engineers simple ideas with jargon; translate "task-relevant maturity" to
  "know the person/agent's skill on this exact task." Don't let the vocabulary obscure it.
- It's a 1983 manufacturing-era lens, light on emotion, culture, and psychological safety —
  pair it with modern people-leadership thinking for human teams.
- Treating human effort as a pure metric to optimize can dehumanize; keep judgment in the loop.
