# Power and Progress — Acemoglu & Johnson's frameworks, as a skill

Source: *Power and Progress: Our Thousand-Year Struggle Over Technology and Prosperity*
(Daron Acemoglu & Simon Johnson, 2023). This skill lets you **apply** the book's lens to
real technology decisions, not just describe it. Extracted by Managing Machines, Issue 026.

## When to use
- Deciding whether a tool or agent should **automate** a task away or **augment** the human doing it.
- Evaluating a rollout: who actually captures the productivity gains (workers, users, capital)?
- Designing AI agents/tooling and choosing between replacing people and making them more capable.
- Pressure-testing a vendor's or frontier lab's "vision" of where the technology should go.
- Assessing the labor, distributional, or morale effects of an automation decision.

## Core principle
**There is nothing automatic about new technology bringing widespread benefit.** Output and
who captures it are two different questions. The first is technical; the second is decided by
power, institutions, and the *direction* technology is pushed. So the job is to choose the
direction deliberately — augment by default, automate when it genuinely clears the bar — and
to design who shares the gains.

## The frameworks

**The Productivity Bandwagon** — the belief that more productivity automatically lifts wages
and jobs. *Procedure:* before assuming a tech "lifts everyone," check the two conditions —
(1) does it create new tasks that raise labor's marginal product, and (2) do workers have the
power to bargain for a share? If either is missing, the bandwagon stalls and gains pool at the
top. *Use when* someone argues a rollout will benefit everyone by default.

**Automation vs. New Tasks** — the central fork. *Procedure:* classify the change — does it
*substitute* a machine for a worker's task (automation → income shifts to capital), or does it
*create new things workers can do* / make them more productive (augmentation → wages rise)?
Aim for a mix weighted toward new tasks. *Use when* scoping any tool, feature, or agent.

**So-So Automation** — automation only marginally better than the human it replaces.
*Procedure:* estimate the true end-to-end productivity gain vs. the worker, net of oversight,
error-handling, and integration costs. If the gain is small but you replace the human anyway,
it's "so-so" — big distributional loss, tiny output gain. Reject it; keep the human and
augment. *Use when* an automation case rests mostly on cutting the wage/headcount bill.

**Machine Usefulness (vs. Machine Intelligence)** — build tools that make humans more capable
and better-informed rather than autonomous systems that imitate and replace them. *Procedure:*
ask "does this raise what a person can do?" before "can this do the person's job?" Prefer
designs that supply information, options, and reach to a human in the loop. *Use when* setting
a product or R&D direction.

**Power, Vision & the Vision Oligarchy** — power is the ability to achieve your objectives,
including via persuasion; a narrow elite shapes the dominant vision of "progress." *Procedure:*
name whose vision a roadmap encodes and whose objectives it serves; ask what alternatives that
vision suppresses; form an explicit counter-vision for your own context. *Use when* a "this is
just where the technology is going" argument is steering a decision.

**Redirecting Technology (countervailing power)** — the remedy when direction is captured.
*Procedure:* identify the levers that can bend the path — incentives/taxes, regulation,
worker/user voice, antitrust, data rights, and funding a pro-human alternative. Apply the
organizational analogues you control (review gates, charter, who-shares-the-gains rules).
*Use when* you need to change the default trajectory, not just complain about it.

## How to apply — decision procedure
1. **Separate the two questions.** What's the real output gain, and *separately*, who captures it?
2. **Place it on the fork.** Automation or new-task/augmentation? Push toward augmentation.
3. **Run the so-so test.** Net end-to-end gain vs. the human, after oversight and error costs. If small, don't replace — augment.
4. **Apply machine usefulness.** Choose the design that raises what people can do over the one that imitates them.
5. **Check the bandwagon conditions.** New tasks created? Workers/users able to claim a share? If not, design the redistribution in.
6. **Name the vision.** Whose objectives does the chosen path serve, and what alternative are you skipping? Write your counter-vision.
7. **Pull a redirection lever.** Set the gate, charter, or gain-sharing rule that bends the default toward broad benefit.

## In the agentic world order
- **Automate vs. augment is your weekly choice.** Every agent replaces a task or amplifies a
  person. Default to augmentation — agents that brief, draft, retrieve, and check humans — and
  reserve full automation for tasks where the end-to-end gain is large and verifiable.
- **Hunt your "so-so" agents.** An agent that replaces a worker while barely beating them
  destroys value: you pay oversight, error, and morale costs for a tiny output bump. Measure
  end-to-end quality, not headcount removed. So-so → keep the human and augment.
- **Machine usefulness over mimicry.** Build agents that make your people more capable and
  better-informed, not ones whose only pitch is "it can do their job."
- **Watch the bandwagon inside your org.** Agent productivity only lifts the team if you
  reinvest freed time into higher-value new tasks and share the upside; otherwise the surplus
  pools and your best people leave. The redistribution is a management decision, not automatic.
- **Don't outsource your vision.** The frontier-lab/vendor roadmap is one vision optimized for
  their objectives. Keep a counter-vision: decide which tasks stay human, instrument who
  captures the gains, and treat "the model can do it" as the start of the argument, not the end.

## Pitfalls
- The authors' own caution: **progress is not automatic** — assuming an agent rollout
  benefits everyone by default is exactly the bandwagon error the book warns against.
- **So-so automation is seductive** because it cuts the wage bill immediately; the costs
  (oversight, errors, lost expertise) show up later and off the headline metric.
- Fair criticism to keep honest (Noah Smith and others): the automate/augment "menu" can be a
  *just-so story* — don't infer a technology's type only from its wage outcome; examine the
  actual mechanism first. The history in the book is selective, so treat its sweeping
  generalizations as hypotheses, not proof.
- Defining **power to include persuasion risks tautology** ("they won because they had power").
  Use the framework to find concrete levers, not to relabel outcomes you already observed.
- The book's policy remedy (experts steering a "menu" of technologies) **assumes foresight
  that history rarely rewards** — apply redirection humbly, with feedback and reversibility,
  not central-planner confidence.
