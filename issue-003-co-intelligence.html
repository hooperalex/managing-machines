Today's Book
Co-Intelligence
by Ethan Mollick  ·  2024  ·  Technology / Business
"The AI you are using right now is the worst AI you are ever going to use."
⏱ 6-min read   ★ 3.9 / 5   256 pages
Read this if… you want a calm, practical field guide to working with generative AI — not a hype manifesto and not a doom sermon. A Wharton professor who actually uses these tools every day shows you how to fold them into real work.
01 — Key Takeaways
1.Invite AI to the table. The fastest way to learn what AI can do is to try it on everything you legally and ethically can. You can't intuit the capability boundary — you have to probe it by doing.
2.Stay the human in the loop. AI is a confident, fluent liar. Keep a person accountable for judgment, fact-checking, and the final call — especially where the cost of error is real.
3.The frontier is jagged. AI is shockingly good at some hard things and hopeless at some easy ones, with no obvious line between them. Map the jaggedness for your tasks empirically.
4.Treat it like a person — then cast it. Talking to AI like a collaborator works better than treating it like software. But you must tell it what kind of person to be — the persona shapes the output.
5.Assume this is the worst AI you'll ever use. Today's model is the floor, not the ceiling. Build habits and systems that get better as the models do, instead of betting on today's limitations holding.
6.Centaur or cyborg, not bystander. Either split tasks cleanly between you and AI (centaur) or interleave moment-to-moment (cyborg) — but co-work with it rather than handing over or refusing.
02 — Core Concepts

The Four Rules — Mollick's operating principles: (1) Always invite AI to the table; (2) Be the human in the loop; (3) Treat AI like a person, but tell it what kind of person it is; (4) Assume this is the worst AI you will ever use.

The Jagged Frontier — AI's capabilities form an uneven boundary: tasks of similar apparent difficulty fall on opposite sides of "AI can / AI can't," and the line is invisible until you test. Skill comes from knowing the terrain.

Centaurs & Cyborgs — two modes of human-AI collaboration. The centaur divides labor by strength (you do this, AI does that). The cyborg blends the two fluidly within a single task, passing work back and forth.

Alien Intelligence — LLMs aren't human minds and aren't conventional software; they're a genuinely new, "alien" kind of intelligence trained to predict text. Anthropomorphize for usefulness, but don't be fooled about what's underneath.

The Roles of AI — Mollick's working frame: AI as a person, creative, coworker, tutor, coach, and a lens on the future. Each role is a different way to put the same tool to work.
03 — The Book, Part by Part

Setup — Three Sleepless Nights & Creating/Aligning Alien Minds. How LLMs are built, why they feel alien, and the alignment problem — getting these systems to do what we actually want.

The Four Rules for Co-Intelligence. The practical heart of the book: the four principles above, the jagged frontier, and the centaur/cyborg models for collaboration.

AI as a Person / Creative / Coworker. Using AI as a thinking partner, an idea generator and first-draft engine, and a colleague that reshapes how knowledge work gets done.

AI as a Tutor / Coach. The most optimistic chapters: AI as a personalized, patient teacher and a coach for skills and growth — Mollick's home turf as an educator.

AI as Our Future & Epilogue: AI as Us. Scenarios for where this goes, why he declines to forecast doom or utopia, and a closing reflection on what co-intelligence means for being human.

05 — The Other Side  ·  Opinions, Criticism & What to Read Next

Why people love it: an instant New York Times bestseller and a Best Book of the Year pick from The Economist and the Financial Times. Mollick is calm, credible, and refreshingly non-hysterical — the rare AI book that is neither breathless boosterism nor existential panic, written by someone who genuinely experiments with the tools daily. The four rules are sticky and usable on Monday morning.

Fair criticisms: if you've already used AI seriously, much of it feels obvious — anecdote-driven and thin on depth. As a 2024 book about a field that moves monthly, parts already read as dated (it predates today's reasoning and agentic models). Critics note it largely sidesteps existential and labor-displacement risk by design, which can feel evasive, and the relentless optimism papers over harder questions about accuracy, jobs, and harm.

Read next: The Coming Wave (Mustafa Suleyman) for the containment-and-risk view Mollick mostly skips; Power and Progress (Acemoglu & Johnson) for the political economy of who wins from automation; and The Worlds I See (Fei-Fei Li) for the science and humanity behind modern AI.
⚙ In the Agentic World Order
How this book holds up when AI agents do the work.

Invite the agent to the table — everywhere. Mollick's "try AI on everything" becomes a fleet mandate: route a slice of every workflow through an agent to discover, empirically, where it adds value. You map capability by deploying, not by guessing.

Human in the loop → human on the loop. With one chatbot you sit in the loop. With a fleet you can't — so you move onto it: design review gates, sampling, and approval checkpoints so judgment stays accountable without you reading every output.

Map the jagged frontier as an eval suite. The frontier is the whole game for an agent fleet. Turn "where does it succeed/fail" into a real eval harness per task, so you route each job to the side of the frontier the agent can actually handle.

Cast the persona; assume the floor rises. "Tell it what kind of person it is" is system-prompt and role design at scale. And "worst AI you'll ever use" is the operator's planning assumption: build harnesses, tools, and guardrails that upgrade for free when the next model lands — don't hard-code around today's weaknesses.
